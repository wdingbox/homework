Definition of Scientific Study

The word 'study' is a middle English, shortening of Old French estudie (noun), estudier (verb), both based on Latin studium ‘look at closely' and 'zeal, painstaking (taking or characterized by taking pains or trouble) application.’

The meaning of 'study' and 'research' are similar in that both are to spend effort to look at something closely to make it known clearly. The difference is that study is to acquire what is made known. Whereas research is to establish facts to reach new conclusions through systematic investigation.

The English word "science" appeared in mid-14c, rooted from sci- (scissors), "cut, split, divide, seperate", implied "to distinguish true from false", denoted "what is known acquired by study", "assurance of knowledge, certitude, certainty", as "experiential knowledge;" From 17c.-18c. this concept commonly was used in philosophy. [2]

In far east, the word "science" was first translated by Japanese as "科学" (literally means category knowledge) no later than 1864 in Early Meiji Dynasty(明治初年). In 1897, the same Japanese term began to spread widely in China after publish of the book, The Japanese Catagory Record(日本书目志), which was written by Chinese scholar Kang YouWei (康有为, 1858-1927).

The opposite of science is not anti-science but syncretism which means to amalgamate different categories together. In religion, syncretism means "reconciliation of different beliefs while blending practices of various schools of thought" 1610s, from French syncrétisme (17c.) and directly from Modern Latin syncretismus (used by German Protestant theologian David Pareus, 1615), from Greek synkretismos "union of communities," from synkretizein "to combine against a common enemy," from syn- "together" (see syn-) + second element of uncertain origin. One theory connects it with kretismos "lying," from kretizein "to lie like a Cretan;" another connects it with the stem of kerannynai "to mix, blend;" krasis "mixture." Related: Syncretist; syncretistic .

Syncretism involves the merging or assimilation of several discrete things. Syncretism is different from application in that syncretism makes things obscure and unclear about its orign--it make a thing looks like but is not. While application will always keep its original applied resources clear and unchanged. Syncretism contains secret stealing process. Application contains open buying process. The scientific study means to use the scientific method to conduct their study or research. The scientific method is a standardized way of making observations, gathering data, forming theories, testing predictions, and interpreting results. Three key elements are 1) evidence, 2) theory, and 3) application. Application is to put the deducted theory into operation especially for some human practical purpose. In return, application can produce new evidence and inspire new ideas or theories. Hence, the scientific method is an endless recyclic process of evidencing, theorization and application. Evidencing and theorization can be shortened as 'science'. The practical application of knowledge especially in a particular area is called 'technology'. science-and-technology. Definition of Biblical Study

The term 'biblical study' or 'biblical Christian' are new terms appeared recently in seminary school. The word 'biblical' means anything relating to, of, or being in accord with the Bible. The Bible is specifically refer to the sacred scriptures of Christians comprising 49 books of the Old Testament and 36 books of the New Testament.

The word 'bible' comes from Anglo-Latin biblia in early 14c, rooted from biblio, a collection of books, papers or scrolls. [] The Christian scripture was referred to in Greek as Ta Biblia as early as c. 223. Bible replaced Old English biblioðece (see bibliothec) as the ordinary word for "the Scriptures." Figurative sense of "any authoritative book" is from 1804. Bible-thumper "strict Christian" is from 1870. []

The term "biblical study" cannot be found in the Bible. The closest related phrase in NT appeared only once in NIV translated as "diligently study the Scriptures" (John 5:39) The concept of 'biblical study' may come from the Bible study of the fellowship group where some ordinary people gather together to study the Bible as a personal religious or spiritual practice in Christian communities. The term "biblical study" was coined to distinguish itself from regular study which is scientific study in the world.

Biblical study can be defined as the Christian's Bible study with the enhanced academic format. Academic format means a set of precise rules for education system.

Biblical study primarily differs from the Christians' Bible study in that a) a Bible study occurs in a inner circle of a fellowship group, whereas, a biblical study or research mainly appears in public seminary college or institute. b) a Bible study is light in burden but thick in agape and prayer, biblical study endures pressures academically, financially, socially and spiritually. c) the Bible study seeks balanced spiritual growth in life, biblical study emphasizes on intellect which is the power of knowing as distinguished from the power to feel and to will. Overall, biblical study is not advanced level for Bible study and cannot replace the Bible study in fellowship group.

Differences between Biblical Study and Scientific Study

Biblical study is subject to confuse with regular study in school which is essentially a scientific study. Both are the same as looking into closely, making things clear and acquiring knowledge consciously. Both types of study have learning and growing process which involves the evidencing, theorization and application. In seminary school, both are almost identical in forms of classes, lectures, academic evaluation system, and administration mechanism.

However, both types of study essentially differ to each other. First, the biblical study is driven under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. Its motivation is to seek the eternal truth, the Word. Whereas scientific study is mainly driven by human desires. It has no clear consistant motivation, it could be driven by personal career or amateur, economic profit, any political or religious ideal and so on.

Second, biblical study has a clear missionary driven goal--to know Him and make Him known--to know Him in order to make Him known over the world. It is by knowing Him, each individual can better understand the specific mission that He assigned to him and to prepare to accomplish it. Scientific study may have a short-term goal mainly driven by the world.

Third, biblical study is up and down reciproral communication process between the world and the Heaven. Whereas scientific study involves in an endless trial process between theory and application, science and technology. Biblical study has a terminal endpoint which is the Church where to worship HIM. It is an one-way ticket toward the eternal glorious kingdom of God. Although many believe that the endless process between science and technology is a spiral up evolutionary process, its ultimate end is a black hole.

Even though biblical study is similar to scientifc study in evidencing, theorization and application, essentially in detail they are completely different beings. First, biblical study does not rely on scientific evidence used in scientific study which focuses on the material world. Instead, biblical study primarily relies on biblical testimony. The word "testimony" means "evidence, statement of a witness" first recorded early 15c., from Old French testimonie (11c.), from Latin testimonium "evidence, proof, testimony," from testis "witness". The words "testify" and "testimony" both derive from the Latin word testis, referring to the notion of a disinterested third-party witness. In Christians community, especially within the evangelical tradition, the term "to testify" or "to give one's testimony" is used to mean "to tell the story of how one became a Christian". Commonly it may refer to a specific event in a Christian's life in which they believe God did something deemed particularly worth sharing. Testify and test both are similar in role for evidencing proof.

Many things are not repeatedly testable. Biblical testimony is always unreproducible, unique identical and often supernatural. Biblical testimony transcends the world of scientific evidence. Scientific evidence can be true and false, helpful and harmful for biblical study. Hence, scientific evidence at most can be used as a secondary evidence in biblical study.

Second, theorization in biblical study is always based on the biblical laws which are the commands of God. All theological argumentation principles are derived from the Scripture. In biblical study, there exists a terminal of the chains of reasoning--which is the command from God. Whereas in scientific study, the theorization is based upon some generally acknowledged natural axiom, the certainty of which depend on the endless testing.

Third, biblical study does not have application or technolgy that scientific study pursues. There is no such word or similar concept in the Bible. In biblical study, it is incorrect to try to think to apply biblical testimony or principles into your life like scientific study did, since the application is manipulated by man rather than God.

A Bible Scholar vs A Christian
What is a Bible scholar?
Answer: A scholar is someone who has done advanced study in a special field. Therefore, a Bible scholar would be a person who has done advanced studies in Bible, perhaps by going to seminary or graduate school. Perhaps a “Bible scholar” would be differentiated from a pastor, as the pastor’s primary job is to shepherd the church whereas a “scholar” may work in isolation, writing and doing research. A “Bible scholar” may also be differentiated from a theologian in that a theologian is working to put together a comprehensive system of doctrine whereas a Bible scholar may be content to simply clarify what the Bible says without trying to systematize it. Seminaries today often have separate departments corresponding to the distinctions above. A seminary may have a “Pastoral Studies” department, a “Theological Studies” department, and a “Biblical Studies” department, among others.

Having said that, there is no authoritative, technical standard for what it takes to be a Bible scholar. Some who have never been to seminary but have studied the Bible extensively and availed themselves of good resources may indeed be genuine Bible scholars—they are students of the Bible. Likewise, there need not be a sharp distinction between the various seminary departments mentioned above. We would hope that every pastor and theologian would also be a Bible scholar. One would also hope that every Bible scholar would be able to use the knowledge acquired to minister to people.

Because of the wide variety of approaches to the Bible and the many attacks on the reliability of God’s Word today, it is often necessary to add an extra modifier to Bible scholar. Today, the church is served by many fine evangelical Bible scholars who believe that the Bible is God’s Word and seek to clarify the meaning of the Bible for the good of the church and to the glory of God. Unfortunately, there are many liberal Bible scholars, critical Bible scholars, and even skeptical Bible scholars who believe that the Bible holds no authority, being merely a book of literature or a historical record of the religious experiences of people in the past. These scholars often put themselves in the position of judging the Bible rather than the other way around.

It is interesting that the word scholar can also mean “student”—anyone who is studying at any level. Today, some schools in the United States refer to their students as “scholars”—even Kindergarteners. Using this definition, every Christian could and should be a “Bible scholar.” It is sad that much of the church today is biblically illiterate.

Scripture teaches us the importance of studying what it has to say. This study is not limited to taking a class at a Bible college or seminary or even Sunday School. Christians are supposed to feed on God’s Word in whatever ways are available to them—reading, studying, memorizing, and meditating on the Word; reading good books that help explain the Bible; attending church services where they can hear Bible preaching and teaching; listening to Christian radio; and, of course, utilizing good online tools like Got Questions.

Below are just a few of the verses that speak of the importance of studying God’s Word and being a “Bible scholar”:

“I have hidden your word in my heart that I might not sin against you” (Psalm 119:11).

“I meditate on your precepts and consider your ways. I delight in your decrees; I will not neglect your word” (Psalm 119:15–16).

“This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you shall meditate on it day and night, so that you may be careful to do according to all that is written in it” (Joshua 1:8).

“Now the Berean Jews were of more noble character than those in Thessalonica, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if what Paul said was true” (Acts 17:11).

“Every scripture is inspired by God and useful for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the person dedicated to God may be capable and equipped for every good work” (2 Timothy 3:16–17).

“But he answered, ‘It is written, “Man does not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the mouth of God”’” (Matthew 4:4).

“For whatever was written in the past was written for our instruction, so that we may have hope through endurance and through the encouragement from the Scriptures” (Romans 15:4).

There were a number of Bible scholars who interacted with Jesus on a regular basis. These scholars were called the scribes and Pharisees. The scribes were professionals whose job it was to know the law of God and copy and interpret it for others. The Pharisees were a very strict sect of Judaism who made it their business to know and fastidiously keep all of the laws of God. However, simply knowing the facts of Scripture is not enough.

Jesus warned, “You study the Scriptures diligently because you think that in them you have eternal life. These are the very Scriptures that testify about me, yet you refuse to come to me to have life” (John 5:39–40). The scribes and the Pharisees studied the Scriptures, and that was a good thing. However, their focus became the book, the words, the body of literature. They thought that by knowing the Word of God they would gain eternal life. In their zeal for the Word of God, they missed God Himself. If they had really understood what they were studying, they would have come to Christ, because all Scripture points to Him and is fulfilled in Him. The scholars’ intellectual and legalistic pursuit of God’s Word had blinded them to the very subject that God’s Word attempted to illuminate.

In the final analysis, there is no benefit to being a Bible scholar if the scholar does not submit to the authority of the Bible. There is no benefit to knowing God’s Word if one does not get to know God in the process. The Bible does not give us eternal life, but it points us to Jesus who does. It is difficult to understand who God is and the life that is available in Christ without making it a priority to study the Bible.
